>_ WHY YOU SHOULD NEVER REUSE PASSWORDS (WITH REAL BREACH DATA)

It's tempting to use the same password everywhere. It's easy to remember. But it's also the fastest way to get all your accounts hacked at once.

📉 The Numbers Don't Lie

  • 81% of data breaches are caused by weak or reused passwords (Verizon Data Breach Report)
  • 65% of people reuse passwords across multiple accounts (Google/Harris Poll)
  • 15 billion+ credentials are circulating on the dark web right now

💥 Real Data Breaches (What Happened)

LinkedIn (2012)

6.5 million passwords stolen. Users who reused their LinkedIn password had their email accounts, bank accounts, and social media compromised months later. Attackers automated logins across hundreds of sites.

Adobe (2013)

153 million accounts exposed. Encrypted passwords were cracked easily. Reused passwords led to widespread identity theft and financial fraud.

Yahoo (2013-2014)

3 billion accounts breached. The largest breach in history. Many victims reported their reused passwords gave hackers access to their Amazon, PayPal, and iCloud accounts.

Collection #1 (2019)

773 million emails + 21 million passwords. A single database containing 2.7 billion records — all from reused credentials collected over years. This wasn't one breach — it was the result of millions of people reusing passwords.

🔓 How Credential Stuffing Works

Hackers use automated tools to take leaked passwords from one breach and try them on:

  • Your email account
  • Your bank or PayPal
  • Amazon, eBay, or shopping accounts
  • Social media (Facebook, Instagram, X)
  • Work accounts and VPNs

This is called "credential stuffing". It's automated, fast, and devastating.

🛡️ How to Protect Yourself

  • ✅ Never reuse passwords. Every account gets a unique password
  • ✅ Use a password manager to generate and store unique passwords
  • ✅ Enable 2FA (two-factor authentication) on every account that offers it
  • ✅ Check if your email has been breached at haveibeenpwned.com
  • ✅ Change any passwords you've reused immediately

💡 The Bottom Line

One breach is all it takes. If you reuse passwords, your security is only as strong as the weakest website you've ever signed up for. A password manager makes unique passwords effortless — there's no excuse to reuse anymore.
